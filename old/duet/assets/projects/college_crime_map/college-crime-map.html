College Crime Map

On-Campus College Crime in 2016

  • bubbles display standardized crime rate per 1,000 students
  • click legend to toggle crimes
  • hover over bubbles to display stats

Viewing

Map designed for a laptop or desktop environment, as bubbles display stats when hovered over with a mouse and scrolling allows users to zoom in on specific locations.

Map is responsive to 781px, then only a static png image is displayed. If you are having troubles viewing the map, maximize the webpage, or switch to laptop or desktop if you are currently on mobile.

Map can be reset by clicking the 'reset icon' in the top right coner of the map (only visable on hover).

Data Definitions

On-Campus

The grounds and buildings of a college or university (merriam-webster). No futher details provided by Department of Ed.

Assault

Defined as Aggravated Assult, which according to Wikipedia, typically involves a deadly weapon. However, it is not confirmed in the dataset or data dictionary if a deadly weapon was involved in each case.

Robbery

Taking or attempting to take anything via threat or force (wikipedia). No further clarity provided by Department of Ed.

Fondling

Assumed to include acts of unwelcomed touching or groping. No further information provided by Department of Ed.

Rape

Statutory rape not included in dataset. No further information provided by Department of Ed.

Data Source

Data are made publically available through the clearly act and can be downloaded from the the Department of Education's website. They have a couple different ways of downloading data -- this is the tool I used: https://ope.ed.gov/campussafety/#/datafile/list.

From here, I downloaded the zip file to my local machine and extracted the documents into my working directory. The zip file contains 29 documents comprised of excel sheets and data dictionaries.

2016 is the most recent data availible.

Why On Campus Crime?

Schools are most repsonsible for crimes that occur on their own campuses. How responsible is a school? Resonsibility likely ranges along with the diversity of challenges facing urban and rural campuses. Some schools have a promient campus police presence and the majority of students live on campus. In these situations, schools are very responsible for the crimes that occurs on their campuses, as the crimes are likely commited by students and the cases investigated by campus police.

Crime rates for urban campuses are likely inflated, as cities experience a higher rate of crime at large. There is also no certainity that a crime committed on-campus was commited by a student. However, crimes that occur in on-campus housing facilities are included in the data set.

Why Standardize the Crime Rate?

College enrollments commonly range from a couple hundred students attending liberal arts schools to a few thousand or more enrolled at large public universities. Schools with larger enrollments should have more crime simply because they have more students. Standardizing the crime rate accounts for this difference in enrollment and is a better representation of the probablility of the crime occuring to you.

However, using a standardized rate does have a strong impact on schools with small enrollments located in big cities, like the Massachusetts General Hospital Dietetic Internship located in Downtown Boston. There are twenty two students enrolled in the internship. It seems unlikely that four of these students were assulted, equallying a crime rate of 181.8 assults per 1,000 students, and creating a bubble that is one of the largest on the map. What is more likely is that four assults occurred on the hospital campus, not involving students, and are largely due to factors inherent to Downtown Boston and hospital settings.
